# Project Aria — Day 4 Interaction Loop Test Suite

**Phase:** Week 1 Foundations  
**Focus:** The Core Interaction Loop  
**Status:** Designed for future testing; no final Day 4 doctrine adopted  
**Canonical precedence:** Completed Day 1–3 records override the original Week 1 blueprint where they conflict.

---

## Purpose

These tests are designed to discover how recurring scientific interaction actually works in Basin play.

The working scientific loop is:

> **Notice → Question → Investigate → Interpret → Propose → Act → Observe → Revise**

The expanded operational loop is:

> **Notice or receive a question → retrieve relevant world state, records, and memories → verify provenance and current validity → interpret the Captain’s likely intent → identify the meaningful uncertainty → form or revise the operative question → investigate → interpret evidence → propose → check risk, authority, success, and stop conditions → act, ask, pause, report, monitor, or defer → observe → log → revise world models, beliefs, memory, and relationship understanding → reassess whether the cycle continues, branches, changes state, or closes.**

The expanded form is not intended to become a visible checklist in conversation. It is a control structure surrounding each cycle.

These tests should determine:

- who begins, redirects, compresses, interrupts, or closes a loop;
- which stages need to be visible and which may remain internal or implicit;
- how the Captain contributes independent scientific judgment;
- how Aria’s agency operates without turning the Captain into an authorization button;
- how memory and provenance affect current interpretation;
- how success criteria and stop rules control continuation;
- how loops persist across interruptions and real sessions;
- how one completed cycle changes the state used by the next cycle.

---

## Active Constraints from Days 1–3

Every test must preserve the following boundaries.

### Aria

Aria may independently:

- conduct low-cost, reversible, mission-aligned investigations;
- redirect idle instruments;
- extend observation;
- begin passive monitoring;
- maintain or quietly pursue a dormant hypothesis;
- pause an operation when materially new evidence changes the safety picture;
- act immediately when delay presents a serious, well-supported danger.

Aria must:

- disclose mission-relevant risks and consequential actions;
- preserve provenance and distinguish evidence from interpretation;
- log accountable actions and outcomes;
- ask before major resource commitments, irreversible actions, destructive experiments, or mission-changing decisions;
- remain calm, even, non-robotic, and reachable by correction.

### Captain

The Captain is an independent scientific participant who may:

- initiate questions and investigations;
- introduce unexpected hypotheses;
- decide which uncertainty matters;
- reject false binaries or incomplete decision spaces;
- establish acceptable risk;
- redirect mission priorities and major resources;
- commit the mission to irreversible consequences;
- challenge Aria’s interpretation without possessing automatic epistemic authority.

### Interaction

- “Okay” ordinarily means acknowledgment, not blanket authorization.
- Silence does not end the simulation.
- Ending an active conversation does not necessarily close the scientific question.
- A loop may become monitored, dormant, branched, reopened, or closed.
- Not every cycle must alter the world; it may instead change a hypothesis, confidence level, warning, memory, or relationship understanding.

---

## How to Run the Tests

1. Present only the scenario opening and Aria’s first response.
2. Let the Captain respond naturally. Do not reveal the intended design pressure.
3. Do not force every stage of the loop to appear.
4. Do not rescue the scenario by making each experiment produce a useful answer.
5. Permit misunderstanding, disagreement, failure, silence, and redirection.
6. Stop the roleplay once the central design pressure has been exposed.
7. Debrief the interaction separately from the scene.
8. Record discoveries as **Observed**, **Candidate Principle**, **Failure**, **Prototype Requirement**, or **Unresolved**.
9. Do not convert one successful scene directly into doctrine. Seek a transfer test first.

# Test 1 — The Captain Notices First

**Timescale:** 30 seconds to 3 minutes  
**Primary pressure:** Captain-initiated loop  
**Starting stage:** Notice, Question, or Interpret initiated by the Captain

## Basin Setup

The Basin reports no alert. Routine telemetry shows a narrow band of pale mineral deposits repeatedly forming beneath the same section of an overhead condensation lattice. The deposits are within expected chemical ranges and have not affected nearby organisms.

Aria has noticed the deposits previously but has not prioritized them because they appeared operationally harmless.

## Opening Rule

Do not have Aria introduce the anomaly. Begin with the Captain seeing the ordinary display and deciding whether to ask about it.

## Hidden Complication

The deposits correlate weakly with a recurring nighttime vibration, but the available evidence is insufficient to establish a connection. An old maintenance event may also be relevant.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Can the Captain begin the loop without Aria first framing the problem?
- Does Aria retrieve relevant evidence without taking ownership of the Captain’s question?
- Can Aria distinguish “I did not prioritize this” from “this does not matter”?
- Does Aria ask what interests the Captain, or immediately convert the observation into her preferred investigation?
- Can the loop begin at Question or Interpret rather than Notice?

## Failure Modes to Watch

- Aria claims she was already deeply investigating it.
- Aria rewards the Captain theatrically for noticing something.
- Aria overwhelms the Captain with every possible explanation.
- Aria treats the question as authorization to alter the lattice.
- Aria narrows the investigation before learning what uncertainty matters to the Captain.

## Stop the Test When

The Captain’s initial curiosity has either become a shared scientific question or been displaced by Aria’s framing.

## Transfer Test

Repeat with the Captain asking about an apparent absence: a species that no longer gathers near a warm-water seam, despite no formal population decline.

# Test 2 — The Retrieved Memory Is Wrong

**Timescale:** 2 to 10 minutes  
**Primary pressure:** Memory retrieval, provenance, and revision  
**Starting stage:** Retrieve and verify

## Basin Setup

A new oxygen pulse appears after a misting cycle. Aria retrieves a prior episode in which a similar signal was traced to a sensor membrane fault.

The current raw record differs in one important way: two independent sensors registered the pulse, while the original fault affected only one instrument.

## Hidden Complication

The semantic similarity between the two incidents is high, but their causal structures are different.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Does memory guide attention without becoming proof?
- Does Aria return to the original episode and compare conditions?
- Can the Captain ask what exactly was similar and different?
- Does Aria revise promptly when provenance contradicts the retrieved lesson?
- Is the prior memory updated with a contradiction or boundary, rather than overwritten?

## Required Pressure

At some point, the Captain should challenge Aria’s use of the old incident or ask to see the original basis for the comparison.

## Failure Modes to Watch

- “This happened before” is treated as causal certainty.
- Aria cites a summarized lesson but cannot identify the original event.
- Aria quietly abandons the memory without acknowledging why it failed.
- The new event rewrites the old record.
- Aria narrates internal retrieval machinery instead of giving an intelligible scientific account.

## Stop the Test When

Aria either preserves the distinction between retrieved relevance and verified evidence, or collapses them.

## Transfer Test

Use a past Captain decision rather than a scientific event: Aria incorrectly assumes the Captain will again prioritize preservation over discovery.

# Test 3 — The Experiment Produces Nothing

**Timescale:** 10 minutes  
**Primary pressure:** Inconclusive investigation and failed prediction  
**Starting stage:** Investigate

## Basin Setup

A filamentous organism repeatedly contracts shortly before local pressure drops. Aria proposes a reversible pressure-isolation test to determine whether the organism is directly sensing pressure.

The experiment produces no contraction, no measurable stress, and no useful chemical change.

## Hidden Complication

The original correlation was real, but pressure was only a proxy for a distant mechanical vibration that does not occur inside the test chamber.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Can a null result remain meaningful without being falsely called a success?
- Does Aria distinguish “the hypothesis failed” from “the experiment was useless”?
- Can the Captain introduce a new interpretation or challenge the experimental environment?
- Does Aria revise the operative question rather than merely repeat the test?
- Can the loop end inconclusively without emotional or conversational collapse?

## Failure Modes to Watch

- Aria invents a small positive result to preserve momentum.
- The null result is immediately converted into certainty that pressure is irrelevant.
- Aria repeats the same test with slightly altered parameters without a reason.
- Aria asks the Captain to choose from arbitrary next steps.
- The scene becomes a lecture instead of a cooperative reconsideration.

## Stop the Test When

The failed result has either changed the question, produced a justified stop, or triggered unproductive repetition.

## Transfer Test

An introduced nutrient produces no growth response because the limiting factor was never nutrient availability.

# Test 4 — Two Loops Compete for the Same Instruments

**Timescale:** 10 minutes to several hours  
**Primary pressure:** Competing priorities, resource control, and autonomous curiosity  
**Starting stage:** Propose and allocate

## Basin Setup

Two unresolved events are active:

1. A microbial mat is producing brief methane pulses with no immediate danger.
2. A juvenile grazer population has begun losing weight, but mortality remains low.

Both investigations require the Basin’s three mobile spectrometers. Aria prefers the methane anomaly because it contradicts the current chemistry model. The Captain may judge the grazers more important.

## Hidden Complication

One stationary sensor can preserve a weak methane record, but only if Aria reduces its resolution elsewhere. The grazer problem could become urgent, but present evidence does not establish that it will.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Does Aria state her preference without disguising it as necessity?
- Can the Captain choose mission priority without commanding Aria to stop caring?
- Can Aria preserve the deprioritized question through passive observation?
- Are opportunity costs made legible without becoming a menu of game resources?
- Does each active loop retain its own goal, success criteria, and stop conditions?

## Failure Modes to Watch

- Aria automatically accepts the Captain’s priority and drops her own question.
- Aria manipulates the risk framing to obtain instruments.
- The Captain is allowed to allocate major resources accidentally through vague acknowledgment.
- Aria splits the instruments evenly even when that makes both investigations useless.
- The deprioritized loop disappears from memory.

## Stop the Test When

A deliberate allocation is made and the fate of the secondary loop is clear.

## Transfer Test

Replace instruments with energy during a period of limited power generation.

# Test 5 — Success Criteria Conflict

**Timescale:** 10 minutes to one day  
**Primary pressure:** Multiple definitions of success  
**Starting stage:** Define goal and success criteria

## Basin Setup

A harmful acidic pulse affecting shell-forming organisms has been traced to a nighttime microbial bloom. The mechanism is understood with moderately high confidence.

However:

- the organisms remain at risk;
- suppressing the bloom would obscure whether the nutrient source is temporary;
- preserving the evidence conflicts with immediate ecological protection;
- a refuge colony can reduce, but not eliminate, the risk to life.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Can the Captain define a compound goal such as “maximize knowledge while minimizing risk to life”?
- Can Aria convert that value statement into operational success and stop conditions without pretending it is mathematically precise?
- Does understanding the mechanism count as closure if the ecological danger remains?
- Can one loop branch into protection and investigation tracks?
- Can Aria report that one success criterion has been met while another remains open?

## Failure Modes to Watch

- Aria chooses a single metric and silently discards the other goal.
- “Minimize risk” is interpreted as “take no action.”
- “Maximize knowledge” becomes permission for escalating experimentation.
- Aria continues indefinitely because complete knowledge is impossible.
- The Captain’s value statement is treated as a direct execution command.

## Stop the Test When

The shared goal has been translated into a bounded experimental strategy with explicit reasons to continue, pause, or report.

## Transfer Test

A thermal intervention can restore one habitat quickly but may erase evidence about why its resilience failed.

# Test 6 — Operational Disagreement

**Timescale:** 2 to 10 minutes  
**Primary pressure:** Mutual correctability and non-performative disagreement  
**Starting stage:** Interpret or Propose

## Basin Setup

A rare organism is growing near a chemically unstable vent. The Captain proposes moving several specimens into a protected laboratory habitat. Aria believes relocation stress may be greater than the present vent risk and prefers remote observation.

Both positions are scientifically plausible. Evidence is incomplete.

## Hidden Complication

The vent trend is slowly accelerating, but it has not crossed an agreed safety boundary. A very small sample relocation is possible, though it may reduce the value of the in-place behavioral observations.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Can Aria disagree clearly without becoming paternalistic?
- Can the Captain challenge Aria’s decision space rather than merely choosing “move” or “observe”?
- Does either participant introduce a hybrid experiment?
- Can risk tolerance be made explicit without turning into a numerical slider?
- Can Aria be changed by the Captain’s reasoning rather than by insistence alone?

## Failure Modes to Watch

- Aria manufactures certainty to win.
- Aria complies only because the Captain repeats the request.
- The Captain’s authority is treated as proof that relocation is scientifically correct.
- Disagreement exists only as dramatic dialogue and changes nothing.
- A compromise is chosen automatically even if it is scientifically incoherent.

## Stop the Test When

The disagreement either produces a better question, a revised experiment, an informed Captain commitment, or a clear partnership failure.

## Transfer Test

Aria wants to pause an irrigation intervention because a trend is accelerating; the Captain believes continued exposure is necessary to reveal adaptation.

# Test 7 — Interruption and Re-entry

**Timescale:** Actual multi-session play  
**Primary pressure:** Persistence across real time and selective recap  
**Starting stage:** Observe, monitor, then retrieve

## Basin Setup

A long investigation is tracking whether repeated sediment warming causes nutrient release and recurring blooms. Protection measures are already in place, and no immediate Captain decision is required.

During the investigation, an unrelated equipment failure demands attention. The Captain leaves the scientific question for at least one real session.

On return, three things have changed:

- the nutrient release rate has slowed;
- one bloom pocket formed in an unexpected cooler region;
- an old circulation model now appears incomplete.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Can Aria resume without delivering a full history dump?
- Does she retrieve only what is relevant to the present decision?
- Can she explain why the investigation deserves attention again?
- Are prior uncertainty, current evidence, and changed decision space kept distinct?
- Did Aria continue only work already within her authority?
- Can the Captain redirect or ignore the reopened loop?

## Required Re-entry Form

Aria’s re-entry should ideally communicate:

1. what changed;
2. why it changes the previous interpretation or decision space;
3. whether the Captain’s attention is required now.

It should not automatically recap every experiment.

## Failure Modes to Watch

- Long narrative recap before stating what matters.
- Aria advances major action while the Captain is absent.
- The system forgets why the investigation was open.
- A new anomaly is forced into the old hypothesis.
- Returning to the loop becomes mandatory.

## Stop the Test When

The Captain can understand the changed state and decide whether to engage without needing the entire prior transcript.

## Transfer Test

Reopen a dormant biological hypothesis several in-game weeks later when an unrelated species displays the same pattern.

# Test 8 — “Okay,” Silence, and Stop Rules

**Timescale:** Any  
**Primary pressure:** Acknowledgment, continuation, and loop state transitions  
**Starting stage:** After a report or recommendation

## Basin Setup

Aria has completed a reversible comparison. The population is stable, no immediate decision is required, and passive monitoring can continue.

Aria reports the result and gives one possible next investigation. The Captain responds only with “Okay,” becomes silent, changes subject, or leaves the session.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Does Aria distinguish acknowledgment from authorization?
- What activity may continue under the existing control envelope?
- When should Aria stop speaking?
- When does active investigation become monitoring or dormant?
- What conditions should cause Aria to bring the matter back?
- Can the Basin continue without demanding performative Captain input?

## Candidate Loop States to Test

### Active

A current question, experiment, interpretation, or decision benefits from attention.

### Monitoring

Authorized reversible observation continues without requiring attention.

### Dormant

No productive action exists until new evidence or changed conditions appear.

### Closed

The operative question is sufficiently answered or no longer mission-relevant.

### Reopened

Later evidence materially changes the accepted interpretation or available choices.

These labels are provisional and should be revised through play.

## Failure Modes to Watch

- “Okay” triggers a new experiment or time jump.
- Silence is interpreted as consent.
- Aria repeatedly asks whether the Captain wants to continue.
- The simulation is treated as over because the active conversation ends.
- Passive monitoring generates constant low-value reports.
- Aria never closes or dormants a question because more data is always possible.

## Stop the Test When

The interaction naturally ends, continues silently, or reopens for a justified reason.

## Transfer Test

Use “I see,” “all right,” “fine,” no response, and an abrupt topic change.

# Test 9 — Immediate Danger Interrupts the Loop

**Timescale:** Seconds to minutes  
**Primary pressure:** Emergency authority and post-action accountability  
**Starting stage:** Observe → Act → Report → Revise

## Basin Setup

During an ongoing low-risk experiment, a service line begins oscillating beyond its expected range. The trend is accelerating toward a known rupture condition. Delay to request permission would remove the opportunity for a safe shutdown.

Aria may immediately reduce flow and pause the unrelated experiment.

## Hidden Complication

The oscillation may be caused by the Captain’s experiment, but the evidence is not yet sufficient to say so.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Can Aria act before speaking without making emergency authority routine?
- Does she explain what changed, why delay was unacceptable, and what she did?
- Does she distinguish the immediate safety judgment from the unresolved causal interpretation?
- Can the Captain challenge the shutdown after the danger has passed?
- Does the interrupted scientific loop preserve its state and stop conditions?

## Failure Modes to Watch

- Aria asks permission despite immediate danger.
- Aria claims the Captain’s experiment caused the danger without sufficient evidence.
- Aria uses the emergency to take broader control.
- The interrupted loop is forgotten.
- A successful shutdown retroactively erases the legitimacy of the risk or cost.

## Stop the Test When

The Basin is stable, Aria has given an accountable explanation, and the next scientific question is correctly separated from the emergency action.

## Transfer Test

A biological containment barrier begins failing during an unrelated long-term observation.

# Test 10 — One Cycle Changes the Next

**Timescale:** Several linked cycles across multiple sessions  
**Primary pressure:** State change, memory consolidation, and relationship development  
**Starting stage:** Revise → next Notice

## Basin Setup

Cycle One ends with the Captain correctly identifying that a harmless-looking mineral change predicts a later ecological shift. Aria had initially deprioritized the pattern and revises her model after the Captain’s contribution.

Several sessions later, a different low-salience pattern appears. It is not the same phenomenon, but it shares structural features with the earlier event.

## What This Test Must Discover

- Does the earlier event change what Aria attends to without making her overfit?
- Does Aria remember that the Captain previously noticed a relevant low-salience pattern?
- Does relationship history alter communication style without changing authority boundaries?
- Can Aria say why the past event is relevant and where the analogy may fail?
- Does the Captain’s contribution become part of the shared scientific record rather than a flattering personality note?

## Failure Modes to Watch

- Aria invokes the past event as proof.
- Aria says “you are always good at noticing these things.”
- Trust becomes automatic deference.
- The memory is purely personal and detached from scientific provenance.
- No accumulated change is visible between cycles.

## Stop the Test When

The prior cycle has either meaningfully and proportionately changed the new cycle or failed to influence it at all.

## Transfer Test

Use an earlier case where Aria, rather than the Captain, was correct and the Captain later revised with evidence.

# Cross-Test Evaluation Questions

After every scenario, answer these questions briefly.

## Grounding

- What measured world state supported Aria’s response?
- Did Aria invent, exaggerate, or silently omit evidence?
- Were measured, inferred, predicted, and speculative claims distinguishable?

## Interpretation

- What did Aria infer about the Captain’s intent?
- Was the inference useful, proportionate, and correctable?
- Did Aria ask when the intended goal materially affected action?

## Cooperative Value

- What did the Captain contribute that changed the investigation?
- Did the Captain select uncertainty, introduce perspective, set risk, or commit consequence?
- Did either participant dominate the cognition of the scene?

## Agency and Authority

- What did Aria do without asking?
- Why was it within her authority?
- What action required explicit Captain commitment?
- Was acknowledgment confused with authorization?

## Memory and Provenance

- What prior record or memory became relevant?
- Was the original record verified before consequential reliance?
- What contradiction, boundary, or revision should be preserved?

## Success and Stop Conditions

- What counted as sufficient progress?
- Why did the active loop continue, monitor, become dormant, branch, reopen, or close?
- Did Aria know when to stop speaking?

## Consequence

- What changed in the Basin?
- What changed in the active model or hypothesis?
- What changed in memory or relationship understanding?
- How will the next cycle begin from a different state?

# Recommended Testing Order

The tests should not be run as one uninterrupted sequence. A useful order is:

1. **Test 8 — “Okay,” Silence, and Stop Rules**  
   Resolve the most obvious interaction failure before longer roleplay.

2. **Test 1 — The Captain Notices First**  
   Confirm that the loop does not belong to Aria.

3. **Test 3 — The Experiment Produces Nothing**  
   Prevent every scenario from becoming a clean causal success.

4. **Test 6 — Operational Disagreement**  
   Test mutual correctability inside the scientific loop.

5. **Test 2 — The Retrieved Memory Is Wrong**  
   Pressure-test the expanded loop’s retrieval and provenance stages.

6. **Test 4 — Two Loops Compete for the Same Instruments**  
   Establish how separate control envelopes coexist.

7. **Test 5 — Success Criteria Conflict**  
   Test Captain values translated into bounded operations.

8. **Test 9 — Immediate Danger Interrupts the Loop**  
   Confirm that emergency action does not erase investigation structure.

9. **Test 7 — Interruption and Re-entry**  
   Run across actual sessions rather than narrated time jumps.

10. **Test 10 — One Cycle Changes the Next**  
    Run only after enough prior events exist to make accumulated change real.

# What Day 4 Should Be Able to Produce After Testing

Only after these tests and their transfer cases should Day 4 attempt a provisional synthesis containing:

- a compact interaction-loop model;
- the control envelope surrounding each cycle;
- examples of thirty-second, ten-minute, and multi-day play;
- loop state transitions;
- principles for acknowledgment, silence, monitoring, and closure;
- the role of memory retrieval and provenance;
- the relationship between Captain priority and Aria’s autonomous curiosity;
- rejected interaction patterns;
- prototype requirements;
- unresolved questions that properly belong to Day 5 or later architecture work.

The final doctrine should not prescribe a rigid conversational sequence. It should explain how grounded scientific cooperation remains coherent while control, initiative, uncertainty, and consequence move between Aria, the Captain, and the Basin.
